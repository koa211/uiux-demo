Quality as a Journey

From artisan workshops to AI-assisted testing, QA has evolved from end-of-line checks to a culture of continuous quality.

Scroll ↓

1) Pre-Industrial Craftsmanship

Era: Before mass production

  • Individual artisans built products end-to-end and inspected as they worked.
  • Quality hinged on skill, reputation, and apprenticeship—no formal standards.
  • Defects were corrected immediately by the same person who created them.

Quality lived inside the craft itself. With end-to-end ownership, there was little separation between making and verifying.

Then

  • One maker, one process
  • Implicit standards
  • Reputation = quality signal

Now

  • Specialized roles & handoffs
  • Documented standards
  • Metrics & traceability

2) Industrial Revolution

Era: Late 1800s–Early 1900s

  • Mass production split labor; dedicated inspectors checked finished goods.
  • Scientific Management (Taylor): procedures, time studies, standard work.
  • Defects surfaced late → scrap, rework, and waste.

Scale improved output, but inspection was mostly end-of-line with weak feedback loops back into production.

Then

  • Post-production inspection
  • Throughput over learning
  • Quality team as gate

Now

  • In-process checks
  • Continuous feedback
  • Quality as a shared duty

3) Statistical Quality Control

Era: 1920s–1940s

  • Shewhart pioneers SPC: control charts, sampling plans, capability indices.
  • WWII demands push reliability at scale; data informs interventions.
  • Shift from 100% inspection to sampling and early variation detection.

For the first time, data rather than intuition drove process control and decision making about quality.

Then

  • Judgment calls
  • All-or-nothing checks
  • Late defect discovery

Now

  • Control charts & alerts
  • Sampling strategies
  • Preventive adjustments

4) Total Quality & Standards

Era: 1950s–1970s

  • Deming & Juran: quality is everyone’s responsibility; management systems matter.
  • Kaizen & quality circles: team-led, continuous improvement.
  • ISO and industry standards codify expectations and audits.

Quality moved from inspection to improvement culture—process, people, and standards aligned to reduce defects upstream.

Then

  • Departmental QA
  • Reactive fixes
  • Ad-hoc practices

Now

  • Org-wide ownership
  • Continuous improvement
  • Certified processes

5) Software Quality Emerges

Era: 1970s–1980s

  • Software adopts QC ideas; testing mostly after coding (“test at the end”).
  • IEEE/DoD begin publishing software quality standards and guidance.
  • Documentation, code reviews, and acceptance test plans formalize practice.

Late testing caught show-stoppers but was costly; organizations recognized the need to plan for quality—not just test for it.

Then

  • Big-bang testing
  • Manual heavy
  • Late requirements checks

Now

  • Planned test strategy
  • Reviews & static analysis
  • Defect prevention mindset

6) Shift Left & Process Models

Era: 1990s–2000s

  • From Waterfall to Spiral/RUP; QA begins at requirements and design.
  • Risk-based testing, test management tools, formal inspections.
  • Automation starts growing but manual remains significant.

Moving quality activities earlier slashed the cost of fixes and raised clarity on requirements and risks.

Then

  • QA after dev
  • Late risk discovery
  • Tooling siloed

Now

  • QA from the start
  • Risk-based planning
  • Integrated test management

7) Agile, CI/CD, Test Automation

Era: 2001–2010s

  • Agile teams embed QA in sprints; “Quality Engineering” emerges.
  • CI/CD pipelines automate builds, tests, and fast regressions.
  • Unit/integration/E2E frameworks mature (xUnit, Selenium, etc.).

Quality becomes continuous: smaller batches, tighter feedback loops, and broad automation coverage.

Then

  • Stage-gate releases
  • Manual regression suites
  • Slow feedback

Now

  • Trunk-based / CI
  • Automated regression
  • Minutes-level feedback

8) Modern Era: Shift-Left + Shift-Right

Era: 2010s–Now

  • Design-phase QA (accessibility/usability); reviews before code exists.
  • Shift-right: observability, synthetic checks, feature flags in prod.
  • Visual regression, Playwright/Cypress, AI-assisted test generation.
  • Quality spans UX, performance, security, privacy, data integrity.

Modern QA is a product competency: prevent issues early, detect issues late, and learn continuously from real usage.

Then

  • Pre-release only
  • Lab-only data
  • Visual checks by eye

Now

  • Design reviews + prod checks
  • RUM & observability
  • Automated visual diffs & AI assist
